### Calibration weak spot: `email_security_strong`

Of the 313 high-confidence posteriors, all 37 disagreements with the
deterministic pipeline come from `email_security_strong`. The
network's CPT currently assigns substantial posterior mass to
"strong email security" whenever modern mail providers (M365 or
GWS) plus an email gateway are present, even when the actual DMARC
/ DKIM / SPF / MTA-STS configuration is weak.

The synthetic-calibration test
(`validation/synthetic_calibration.py`) flagged this same node
quantitatively before the corpus run; the real-corpus run confirms
it. CPT tuning for `email_security_strong` is the v1.9.1 priority,
specifically:

  - Lower P(strong | M365 + gateway) from 0.75 → ~0.55 unless DMARC
    reject is observed.
  - Add a stronger evidence binding on `dmarc_reject` to push the
    posterior up only when policy is enforcing.
  - Consider splitting into two nodes: `email_security_modern_provider`
    (presence of M365/GWS) and `email_security_policy_enforcing`
    (DMARC reject + DKIM signing).

The other seven nodes are well-calibrated against the deterministic
pipeline at 100%.

## Cross-source conflicts

The corpus produced **zero** domains with cross-source merge
conflicts (`evidence_conflicts` empty for 91/91). Two interpretations:

1. The corpus skews toward consistent enterprise targets where DNS,
   CT, and identity-discovery agree.
2. The conflict-detection thresholds are conservative; some genuine
   minor disagreements may not be reaching the threshold.

The conflict dampener in the Bayesian layer (n_eff penalty) is
exercised by the synthetic and unit tests but did not fire on this
corpus. Future corpus runs should include hardened-target subsets
where conflicts are more likely.

## Multi-signal correlation depth

84 of 91 domains (92.3%) had at least one node fire with more than
one evidence binding. This is the *north-star metric* recon's
correlation work targets ([roadmap.md § Success metrics](../docs/roadmap.md#success-metrics-post-10)):
the share of high-confidence findings whose evidence DAG references
more than one source.

The 7 domains where every node fired with at most one binding are
the hardened-target subset — they trigger sparse=true on most
nodes, which is the calibrated honesty response.

provide enough signal.

## Numerical correctness (publicly reproducible)

The variable-elimination engine matches the Pearl /
Russell-Norvig **Burglary-Earthquake-Alarm** canonical network to
four decimal places under the noisy-likelihood model the schema
enforces:

| Query | Canonical (hard evidence) | recon (noisy likelihood [0.999, 0.001]) |
|---|---|---|
| `P(burglary \| J=T, M=T)` | 0.284 | 0.276 |
| `P(alarm \| J=T, M=T)` | 0.761 | 0.740 |
| `P(earthquake \| J=T, M=T)` | 0.176 | 0.171 |
| `P(burglary \| J=T)` | 0.0163 | 0.0163 |

Deviations from textbook hard-evidence values reflect the schema's
strict-positive likelihood requirement (`(0,1)` open interval),
which produces the noisy-likelihood Bayes update exactly. Reference
in `tests/test_bayesian_canonical.py`.

## CPT sensitivity (publicly reproducible)

`tests/test_bayesian_sensitivity.py` perturbs each CPT entry and
each root prior by ±0.10 across three canonical evidence patterns
(dense M365 stack, dense GWS stack, empty observation):

| Statistic | Posterior shift |
|---|---|
| Median across all perturbations | **0.019** |
| 95th percentile | **0.109** |
| Maximum observed | **0.139** |

A 10% error in any single CPT entry produces at most a ~14% shift
in any posterior — the quantified version of "the model is robust
to small CPT errors". Test failure is a regression guard.

## Synthetic calibration (publicly reproducible)

`validation/synthetic_calibration.py` simulates ground-truth
parameter assignments from the network's joint distribution,
simulates evidence under the likelihoods, and computes Expected
Calibration Error (ECE; [Naeini et al. 2015](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/9602))
on 20,000 synthetic domains:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean Brier (marginal) | 0.134 |
| Sample-weighted ECE (marginal) | 0.247 |
| Mean Brier (conditional on ≥1 binding fired) | 0.164 |
| Sample-weighted ECE (conditional) | **0.160** |

Marginal ECE is high *by design* — the asymmetric-likelihood model
deliberately reports the prior in the no-evidence-fired regime
rather than overclaiming via absence-conditioning (correlation.md
§4.8.3). Conditional ECE is the calibration claim the model
actually makes; per-node breakdown identifies
`email_security_strong` and `aws_hosting` as the calibration weak
spots, both of which need v1.9.x CPT tuning.

## Determinism (publicly reproducible)

`tests/test_v19_robustness.py` verifies float-bit-identical
posteriors across 100 sequential runs and 50 concurrent
`asyncio.gather` runs.

## What this run does NOT validate

1. **Cross-vertical generalization.** The 91-domain sample skews
   toward enterprise (saas-b2b, finance, healthcare). Calibration
   may differ on consumer / niche-SaaS targets where the seed
   network's prior assumptions hold less well.
2. **Hardened-adversarial targets.** Domains with deliberate
   counter-recon hardening were not specifically sampled. The
   sparse-flag rate suggests the layer would handle them
   gracefully, but explicit testing is a v1.9.1 milestone.
3. **Conflict-dampener exercise.** The corpus produced no
   cross-source conflicts. The dampener path is exercised in unit
   and synthetic tests but did not fire on real data here.

## v1.9.x roadmap

Three follow-up validation passes are scheduled:

1. **`email_security_strong` and `aws_hosting` CPT tuning**
   (target: v1.9.1). Driven by both the synthetic ECE per-node
   breakdown and this corpus's spot-check. Re-run this validation
   pass post-tuning; expected: 95%+ overall agreement.
2. **Hardened-adversarial subset** (target: v1.9.2). 50-domain
   sample focused on minimal-DNS / wildcard-cert / heavily-proxied
   targets. Validates that `sparse=true` fires reliably and that
   intervals stay wide where they should.
3. **Topology stability check** (target: v2.0). Validate that
   adding 2–3 nodes (verticals layer? CDN-stack layer?) does not
   destabilize calibration on the existing nodes.


The corpus run and analysis are reproducible with your own private
corpus:

```bash
# Build a private corpus under validation/corpus-private/ (gitignored).
echo "contoso.com" > validation/corpus-private/sample.txt

# Run with --fusion --no-ct --concurrency 6 (be polite).
python -m recon_tool.cli batch validation/corpus-private/sample.txt \
       --ndjson --fusion --no-ct --concurrency 6 \
       > validation/runs-private/v1.9-calibration/results.ndjson

# Anonymized aggregates:
python validation/analyze_v19_calibration.py \
       --input validation/runs-private/v1.9-calibration/results.ndjson
```

The `analyze_v19_calibration.py` script never prints domain names —
only counts, fractions, quartiles, and short SHA-256 hashes for
disagreement triage.
